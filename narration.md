## Slide 5
So, what is an Enrolled Agent? An Enrolled Agent is a person who has earned the privilege of representing taxpayers before the Internal Revenue Service — the IRS. And here's the important part: Enrolled Agents, just like attorneys and CPAs, are generally unrestricted — in which taxpayers they represent, what tax matters they handle, and which IRS offices they appear before.

## Slide 6
Now, why become an Enrolled Agent? Three reasons. First, there's a growing need for representation — rising IRS enforcement means taxpayers need help. Second, tremendous business potential — you can take on as many clients as you can handle, and enhance the brand you work for. And third — this is largely a recession-proof career. People will always have to pay taxes, and they will always need help.

## Slide 7
Next question — who is eligible? You may be surprised by how simple this is.

## Slide 8
Here's how you become an Enrolled Agent. The eligibility is remarkably open: no prior academic qualification is required — you simply need to be over eighteen. The path has five steps: obtain a Preparer Tax Identification Number, apply for the Special Enrollment Examination — the SEE — pass all three parts, apply for enrolment, and clear a suitability check covering your own tax compliance. That's it.

## Slide 9
How long does this take? Around six to nine months to become an Enrolled Agent — and guidance from a good institution helps you stay within that timeframe. After passing all three parts and applying, official enrolment generally takes about ninety days.

## Slide 10
Let's look at the exam itself. Each part of the SEE has one hundred questions — and listen carefully — there is no negative marking. Each part is three and a half hours long, with a total seat time of four hours to allow for a tutorial, a survey, and a fifteen-minute break.

## Slide 11
Is the exam offered year-round? Almost. The test window runs from the first of May to the end of February. March and April are the blackout period, when the test is updated with the latest tax law. So you can appear for the exam ten months in a year — and take the parts in any order you like.

## Slide 12
What's actually covered? Part One is Individuals — taxpayer data, income and assets, deductions and credits, taxation and advice. Part Two is Businesses — business entities, financial information, and specialised returns. Part Three is Representation — practices and procedures before the IRS. And remember, you can take these parts in any order.

## Slide 13
When do you get your result? Within fifteen minutes of finishing, right on your screen — an instant pass or fail. Only you and the IRS see your results. The passing score is one hundred and five. As for cost — there's a two hundred and fifty-nine dollar fee per part, paid when you schedule.

## Slide 14
Once you've passed all three parts, you apply for enrolment within one year of your third exam. You can apply electronically at Pay.gov with the one hundred and forty dollar enrolment fee, or by mail using Form 23. Processing takes around sixty days.
